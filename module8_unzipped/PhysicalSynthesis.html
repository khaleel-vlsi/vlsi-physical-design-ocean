PHYSICAL SYNTHESIS – INTRODUCTION (ZERO → HERO)

1️⃣ What is Physical Synthesis? (Classroom Explanation)

Before we understand Physical Synthesis, let us first understand why it was invented.

When a student learns RTL and Logical Synthesis, the output we get is:

  • Gate-level netlist
  • Logic is correct
  • Timing looks “okay” on paper

But silicon does not work on paper. In real silicon:

  • Gates are placed physically
  • Wires have length, resistance, capacitance
  • Clock does not reach all flops at the same time
  • Power, congestion, noise start affecting timing

Logical synthesis assumes ideal wires | Physical world does NOT have ideal wires

This gap created a huge problem in the industry. So engineers introduced Physical Synthesis.

Definition (Lecture Style): Physical Synthesis is the process of optimizing the design using physical information (placement, wire length, congestion, and timing) to meet power, performance, and area goals before final place-and-route.

In student-friendly English: Physical Synthesis is where logic decisions are made by considering the real physical layout of the chip.

2️⃣ Why Logical Synthesis is NOT Enough?

Because logical synthesis:

  • Does not know where gates will be placed
  • Does not know actual wire length
  • Uses estimated wire delay

After placement, wires become long, timing breaks, and congestion increases. The industry realized: "Fixing timing after placement is too late." Timing must be optimized with physical awareness.

3️⃣ Real-Life Analogy (City Planning)

Logical Synthesis = Designing rooms on paper. Physical Design = Building the city.

On paper, bedroom to kitchen looks close. In reality, bedroom is on 10th floor, kitchen is in another building. You now need longer stairs, elevators, more power, and more time. Designing with a city map in hand is Physical Synthesis.

4️⃣ What Exactly Happens in Physical Synthesis?

  1. Tool takes gate-level netlist
  2. Tool performs initial placement (coarse)
  3. Estimates wire delay, congestion, and timing violations
  4. Performs logic changes (buffer insertion, gate resizing, logic restructuring)
  5. Re-analyzes timing using physical estimates
  6. Iterates until timing improves

5️⃣ Where Physical Synthesis Fits in the VLSI Flow

RTL → Logical Synthesis → Physical Synthesis (YOU ARE HERE) → Placement → CTS → Routing → Signoff.

Physical Synthesis bridges logic and layout, reducing late-stage surprises.

6️⃣ Industry Scenario (Real Engineer Life)

As a physical design engineer, if you wait till routing to fix a -800ps WNS, you'll face schedule slips and too many ECOs. Physical Synthesis improves timing early, leading to fewer ECOs and better power.

8️⃣ Tools and Industry Context

Common tools include Synopsys DC-T, Fusion Compiler, and Cadence Genus. In advanced nodes (28nm → 3nm), wire delay > gate delay, making physical synthesis mandatory.

9️⃣ Inputs Required for Physical Synthesis

Golden Rule: Physical synthesis cannot “guess” silicon reality — it needs correct inputs.
  • Gate-Level Netlist: Structural description from logical synthesis.
  • .lib (Liberty): Timing, power, and functional behavior models.
  • .lef (Library Exchange Format): Physical data like cell size and pin locations.
  • SDC (Synopsys Design Constraints): Clock definitions, delays, and false paths.
  • Floorplan Info: Die area, core area, macro locations, and IO positions.
  • Power Intent (UPF/CPF): Power domains and switches.

🔟 Physical Awareness: Cell Delay vs Wire Delay

In modern nodes, Interconnect delay (Wire delay) dominates timing. Wire delay ≈ R × C. Every extra micron adds delay, which is why placement awareness is critical.

1️⃣1️⃣ Module 4: Floorplan Awareness

Floorplan defines the physical blueprint. Aspect ratio, utilization, IO placement, and macro awareness are key. High utilization (e.g., 95%) is a nightmare for congestion and timing.

1️⃣2️⃣ Physical Synthesis Flow (Step-by-Step)

Step 1: Translation

Converts netlist and libraries into a physically-aware internal database with width, height, and pin locations.

Step 2: Initial Placement Estimation

Rough, timing-driven placement to estimate RC and analyze timing realistically.

Step 3: Optimization Loop

Iterative loop: Buffer insertion, gate sizing, and logic restructuring until timing convergence.

Figure 5: The Detailed Step-by-Step Physical Synthesis Optimization Flow

1️⃣3️⃣ Optimization Techniques

  • Gate Sizing: Swapping small cells for faster, high-drive versions.
  • Buffer Insertion: Breaking long nets to fix slew and delay.
  • Logic Restructuring: Re-balancing logic levels for better path slack.
  • Area Recovery: Downsizing non-critical cells to save power.

1️⃣4️⃣ Timing Closure Concepts

WNS (Worst Negative Slack), TNS (Total Negative Slack), and FEP (Failing Endpoints) are the key metrics. MCMM (Multi-Corner Multi-Mode) ensures the chip works across all voltage and temperature variations.

1️⃣5️⃣ Congestion and Power Awareness

Congestion-aware PS avoids hotspots and spreads logic. Power-aware PS balances high-performance cells on critical paths with low-power cells on non-critical paths.

1️⃣6️⃣ Deliverables (Handoff to P&R)

  • Optimized Gate-Level Netlist
  • Updated SDC Constraints
  • Timing, Power, and Area Reports
  • Physical Guidance Hints

Industry Truth: Physical Synthesis makes P&R easy. P&R makes silicon real.

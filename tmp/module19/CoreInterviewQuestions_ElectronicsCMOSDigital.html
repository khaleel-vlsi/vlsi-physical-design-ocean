BASIC ELECTRONICS

(50 High-Level Interview Questions & Answers – Physical Design Focus)

1. Why is basic electronics important for a Physical Design engineer?

Answer:
 Physical Design decisions directly affect electrical behavior such as delay, power, noise, and reliability. Understanding resistance, capacitance, current flow, and voltage behavior is critical for timing closure, IR drop analysis, and signal integrity.

2. What is resistance and how does it impact timing in PD?

Answer:
 Resistance opposes current flow. In PD, higher interconnect resistance increases RC delay, slowing signal transitions and degrading setup/hold margins.

3. Explain capacitance in the context of VLSI interconnects.

Answer:
 Capacitance exists between metal lines and substrate or neighboring wires. It contributes to delay and coupling noise, impacting signal integrity and timing.

4. What is RC delay and why is it critical in backend design?

Answer:
 RC delay is the product of resistance and capacitance of a net. It determines signal propagation delay and dominates timing at advanced technology nodes.

5. How does wire length affect circuit performance?

Answer:
 Longer wires increase resistance and capacitance, resulting in higher delay, more power dissipation, and increased susceptibility to noise.

6. Why does power increase with switching activity?

Answer:
 Dynamic power is proportional to switching activity because charging and discharging capacitances consumes energy each time a signal toggles.

7. What is Ohm’s Law and how is it used in PD?

Answer:
 Ohm’s Law states V = IR. In PD, it helps estimate voltage drops in power networks and analyze IR drop issues.

8. Define current density and its relevance in routing.

Answer:
 Current density is current per unit area. Excessive current density can cause electromigration, leading to metal failure.

9. What is electromigration (EM)?

Answer:
 Electromigration is the movement of metal atoms due to high current density, causing opens or shorts in interconnects over time.

10. How does temperature affect resistance?

Answer:
 Resistance increases with temperature, which increases delay and worsens IR drop, especially in high-power blocks.

11. Why is capacitance higher in lower metal layers?

Answer:
 Lower metal layers are closer to the substrate and have smaller spacing, increasing parasitic capacitance.

12. What is the difference between lumped and distributed RC models?

Answer:
 Lumped models approximate the entire net as one R and C, while distributed models divide the wire into segments for accurate delay estimation.

13. Why is distributed RC modeling important in timing analysis?

Answer:
 Long interconnects behave like transmission lines, and distributed models give more accurate delay and slew calculations.

14. What is slew rate and why does it matter?

Answer:
 Slew rate is the rise/fall time of a signal. Poor slew increases delay, causes signal integrity issues, and can violate timing constraints.

15. How does capacitance affect power consumption?

Answer:
 Higher capacitance requires more energy to charge and discharge, increasing dynamic power consumption.

16. What is dynamic power equation?

Answer:
 Dynamic power = α × C × V² × f
where α is switching activity, C is capacitance, V is voltage, and f is frequency.

17. What is static power?

Answer:
 Static power is power consumed due to leakage currents even when the circuit is not switching.

18. Why does leakage power increase at advanced nodes?

Answer:
 Thinner oxides, lower threshold voltages, and short channel effects increase subthreshold and gate leakage.

19. What is voltage drop (IR drop)?

Answer:
 Voltage drop occurs due to resistance in the power delivery network when current flows, reducing effective supply voltage.

20. How does IR drop affect timing?

Answer:
 Lower supply voltage reduces cell drive strength, increasing delay and potentially causing setup violations.

21. What is noise margin?

Answer:
 Noise margin is the allowable noise voltage that a signal can tolerate without causing logic failure.

22. How does coupling capacitance cause noise?

Answer:
 Switching activity on one net can induce unwanted voltage changes on adjacent nets through capacitive coupling.

23. What is crosstalk?

Answer:
 Crosstalk is interference caused by capacitive or inductive coupling between neighboring interconnects.

24. Why are shielding and spacing used in routing?

Answer:
 They reduce coupling capacitance, minimize noise, and improve signal integrity.

25. What is decoupling capacitance (decap)?

Answer:
 Decaps provide local charge storage to stabilize supply voltage during switching, reducing IR drop and noise.

26. Where are decaps placed in PD?

Answer:
 Near standard cells, macros, and clock buffers where switching activity is high.

27. What is ground bounce?

Answer:
 Ground bounce is voltage fluctuation on ground due to sudden current changes, causing logic errors.

28. Why is power integrity critical in PD?

Answer:
 Poor power integrity leads to timing failures, functional errors, and long-term reliability issues.

29. What is signal attenuation?

Answer:
 Loss of signal amplitude due to resistive and capacitive effects along interconnects.

30. Why are repeaters used in long nets?

Answer:
 Repeaters break long RC paths, improving delay and signal integrity.

31. What happens if wire resistance is underestimated?

Answer:
 Timing will be optimistic, leading to silicon failures after fabrication.

32. What is parasitic extraction?

Answer:
 The process of calculating resistance and capacitance of interconnects for accurate timing and power analysis.

33. Why does PD rely heavily on extracted parasitics?

Answer:
 Because ideal nets do not reflect real silicon behavior, parasitics determine actual performance.

34. What is power dissipation density?

Answer:
 Power consumed per unit area. High density causes thermal issues.

35. How does heat affect reliability?

Answer:
 Higher temperature accelerates electromigration, leakage, and device aging.

36. What is thermal runaway?

Answer:
 A condition where increasing temperature causes more leakage, which further increases temperature.

37. Why are wider metals used for power routing?

Answer:
 To reduce resistance, lower IR drop, and prevent electromigration.

38. What is voltage scaling and its effect?

Answer:
 Reducing supply voltage lowers power but increases delay and sensitivity to noise.

39. Why is grounding important in chip design?

Answer:
 A stable ground reference ensures correct logic levels and minimizes noise.

40. What is the impact of supply noise on clock networks?

Answer:
 It causes clock jitter and skew, affecting timing closure.

41. Why do backend engineers care about basic circuit theory?

Answer:
 Because layout decisions directly influence electrical behavior and chip performance.

42. What is transient current?

Answer:
 Sudden current spikes during switching that stress the power network.

43. How does metal thickness impact resistance?

Answer:
 Thicker metals have lower resistance and better current handling capability.

44. What is self-heating effect?

Answer:
 Local temperature rise due to power dissipation in dense regions.

45. Why is timing more wire-dominated in advanced nodes?

Answer:
 Transistor delays reduce, but interconnect RC delays increase significantly.

46. What is effective capacitance?

Answer:
 Capacitance experienced by a driver considering coupling and switching activity.

47. Why is low-power design linked with PD?

Answer:
 Placement, routing, and clock design directly affect power consumption.

48. What is reliability vs performance trade-off?

Answer:
 Aggressive optimization improves speed but may increase EM and power issues.

49. How does basic electronics help in ECO fixing?

Answer:
 Understanding RC effects helps in choosing buffer insertion or rerouting strategies.

50. What is the key takeaway of basic electronics for PD interviews?

Answer:
 Physical Design is applied electronics—every layout choice has electrical consequences.

🔹 SECTION A: SHORT CHANNEL EFFECTS (40 QUESTIONS)

1. What are short channel effects (SCE)?

Ans: Deviation from ideal MOSFET behavior when channel length is comparable to depletion regions, reducing gate control.

2. Why do SCE become severe at advanced nodes?

Ans: Drain electric field penetrates into the channel, competing with gate control.

3. Why are SCE unavoidable below 28nm?

Ans: Physical electrostatic limits prevent perfect scaling.

4. What is threshold voltage roll-off?

Ans: Reduction of Vt as channel length decreases.

5. How does Vt roll-off affect leakage?

Ans: Leakage increases exponentially due to reduced barrier height.

6. What is Drain Induced Barrier Lowering (DIBL)?

Ans: Drain voltage lowers source-channel barrier, increasing off-current.

7. Why is DIBL critical for PD engineers?

Ans: Causes VDD-dependent timing and leakage variation.

8. How does DIBL impact timing closure?

Ans: Delay varies across voltage corners, increasing uncertainty.

9. What is subthreshold leakage?

Ans: Diffusion current when VGS < Vt.

10. Why does subthreshold leakage dominate at 7nm?

Ans: Low Vt and strong SCE effects.

11. Why is leakage exponential in nature?

Ans: Governed by Boltzmann carrier distribution.

12. What is punch-through?

Ans: Source and drain depletion regions merge, bypassing gate control.

13. Punch-through vs DIBL?

Ans: Punch-through is catastrophic; DIBL is gradual.

14. How does punch-through affect functionality?

Ans: Device cannot be fully turned off.

15. What layout conditions worsen punch-through impact?

Ans: Dense low-Vt cell placement.

16. What is Gate-Induced Drain Leakage (GIDL)?

Ans: Leakage due to band-to-band tunneling near drain.

17. When does GIDL increase?

Ans: High drain voltage and low gate voltage.

18. Why is GIDL relevant in PD?

Ans: Increases standby power in clock and IO regions.

19. How does temperature affect SCE?

Ans: Higher temperature increases leakage and DIBL.

20. Why does IR drop worsen SCE effects?

Ans: Lower VDD increases delay sensitivity.

21. How does SCE affect noise margins?

Ans: Reduced Vt lowers logic level robustness.

22. Why does SCE cause corner explosion?

Ans: Delay becomes highly sensitive to PVT variations.

23. How does SCE impact hold timing?

Ans: Faster cells worsen hold violations.

24. Why is SCE dominant in always-on blocks?

Ans: Leakage exists even in idle mode.

25. Why is voltage scaling limited today?

Ans: SCE and variability increase at low VDD.

26. How does transistor stacking reduce leakage?

Ans: Reduces effective VDS across each transistor.

27. Why are multi-Vt cells used?

Ans: To balance leakage and performance.

28. How does SCE impact ECO fixes?

Ans: Small changes cause large power/timing shifts.

29. Why is SCE important for CTS?

Ans: Clock buffers are sensitive to Vt variation.

30. Why does SCE worsen at high frequency?

Ans: Increased switching stress.

31. How do FinFETs reduce SCE?

Ans: Gate wraps around channel, improving electrostatics.

32. Why is variability still present in FinFETs?

Ans: Fin width/height variations.

33. Why does SCE affect yield more than speed?

Ans: Leakage failures are harder to guardband.

34. Why is leakage non-uniform across chip?

Ans: Process, temperature, and activity variation.

35. Why does leakage cause thermal runaway?

Ans: Heat increases leakage, forming a loop.

36. How do foundry models capture SCE?

Ans: Advanced BSIM/FinFET models.

37. Why is pessimism higher in sign-off?

Ans: To cover SCE uncertainty.

38. Why is SCE always asked in PD interviews?

Ans: It links device physics to backend decisions.

39. Biggest SCE challenge today?

Ans: Leakage + variability at low VDD.

40. One-line SCE takeaway?

Ans: SCE defines modern PD limits.

🔹 A. DIGITAL ABSTRACTION & LOGIC BEHAVIOR (1–20)

1. Why is digital abstraction still valid even though hardware is analog?

Answer:
 Digital abstraction works because circuits are designed with sufficient noise margins so that small analog variations do not change logical interpretation. Interviewers ask this to see if you understand the limits of digital abstraction.

2. When does digital abstraction break down?

Answer:
 At very low voltage, high frequency, or asynchronous boundaries where noise margins shrink and timing assumptions fail.

3. Why is Boolean logic sufficient to describe complex systems?

 Because any computation can be decomposed into combinations of AND, OR, and NOT, enabling scalable hierarchical design.

4. Why is logic equivalence not the same as timing equivalence?

Answer:
 Two Boolean-equivalent circuits can have very different propagation delays due to different logic depth and structure.

5. Why does logic restructuring change behavior even if truth table is same?

Answer:
 Because internal signal transitions and hazards change, affecting glitches and timing.

6. Why is monotonic logic preferred in some designs?

Answer:
 Because it avoids hazards and unwanted transitions, improving reliability.

7. Why does digital design assume finite transition times?

Answer:
 Zero-delay assumption breaks causality; real designs must consider propagation delay to avoid races.

8. Why is determinism important in digital systems?

Answer:
 Predictable behavior is essential for verification and correctness.

9. Why is logic hierarchy critical for scalability?

Answer:
 It limits complexity and allows independent reasoning about subsystems.

 It destroys locality and increases interconnection complexity.
